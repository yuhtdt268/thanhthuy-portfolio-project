## Post

Your AI articles read fine but say nothing new.

That's because every AI article generator does the same thing: scrape the top 10 Google results and rewrite them. The output is grammatically perfect, well-structured, and completely useless. Zero information gain. Just the same recycled advice in a slightly different order.

After almost two decades of trying to make really good content, here's what finally cracked it.

**The trick isn't better writing. It's better research.**

---

## The Frustrated Reader Method

**Step 1 — Google Top 10: same recycled advice**
A sub-agent roleplays as someone who just Googled your keyword. It reads what's already ranking and asks: *"What am I STILL frustrated about? What didn't these articles answer?"*

Think of it as hiring a really picky reader who tells you exactly where the existing content falls short.

**Step 2 — Frustrated Reader: "What am I STILL missing?"**
The agent identifies the specific gaps — the questions that the top 10 results didn't answer, the nuance they glossed over, the practical details they skipped.

**Step 3 — Research Agent: hunts Reddit + YouTube + Twitter**
A research agent goes hunting in the places where people share what they actually learned — not what ranks on Google. YouTube tutorials, Reddit threads, Twitter discussions.

**Step 4 — Real Insights**
Example: a "2-campaign loop" strategy from a Reddit thread — run a test campaign, find winners, scale them separately. The kind of insight you'd usually only hear at conferences from people who actually scale ad budgets.

**Step 5 — Article That Actually Says Something**
Claude handles the writing section by section. Information gain, not recycled fluff.

**The core principle: Teach AI to be disappointed in Google.**

---

## A Real Example

For a Meta ads article, the core advice ended up being a "2-campaign loop" strategy: run a test campaign, find the winners, then scale them in a separate campaign. That insight came from a Reddit thread — not from the top 10 Google results.

That's the kind of thing that makes an article worth reading and worth citing.
