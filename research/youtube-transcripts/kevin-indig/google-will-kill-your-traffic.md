## Chapter 1: What SEO Actually Is

SEO has historically stood for Search Engine Optimization, but a better definition today is "search everywhere optimization." In one sentence: SEO is getting your website to show up wherever people search for things related to your business.

Google search works in three steps:

1. **Crawling** — Google sends automated bots to read every page on the internet, following links from page to page.
2. **Indexing** — Google stores what those bots find in a massive library. If your page isn't in the index, it can't show up in search results.
3. **Ranking** — When someone searches, Google picks the results it thinks are most relevant, trustworthy, and useful. That order is the ranking, and that's what we're trying to influence.

A modern search results page includes: AI overviews, sponsored ads, organic results, "People also ask" boxes, video carousels, local map packs, and knowledge panels. We've come a long way from 10 blue links.

You shouldn't ultimately have an SEO goal — you should have a business goal (more customers, more revenue, more leads). SEO is one of the most reliable ways to get there because you're reaching people at the exact moment they're looking for what you sell. The key difference is **intent** — someone who Googles "best proposal software for agencies" is actively looking for a solution.

## Chapter 2: Mastering Search Intent

Before keyword research or any tactics, you need to understand the single most important concept in SEO: **search intent**. Every time someone types something into Google, they're looking for something specific. Your job is to figure out what that is and give it to them.

There are four types of search intent:

- **Informational** — "How to train your puppy." They want to learn something.
- **Commercial** — "Best dog training classes." They're willing to spend money but comparing options.
- **Transactional** — "Dog training class near me sign up." They're ready to buy.
- **Navigational/Branded** — "PetSmart dog training." They're using Google to navigate to a specific brand.

How do you figure out search intent? Simple: Google your keyword and look at what's ranking. That's what Google thinks the intent is. The top 10 results are Google telling you exactly what the searcher wants.

**Rule of thumb:** The shorter the keyword, usually the more competitive, higher volume, and less useful it is. "Coffee" gets 650,000 searches a month but the SERP is a mess — every major site is competing for it. "Best dark roast coffee beans for espresso" has lower volume but crystal-clear intent and manageable competition. These longer, more specific phrases are called **long-tail keywords**.

**Search intent in the AI era:** AI overviews now appear across all types of searches, including commercial and transactional. Even when someone is comparing products or ready to buy, Google might answer them before they click a single link. This is called zero-click marketing. Your content needs to go deeper than whatever that summary covers to earn the click. Surface-level content will not cut it anymore.

## Chapter 3: Finding the Right Keywords

A keyword is any phrase someone types into Google — it can be one word or twenty. Your job is to figure out which phrases matter for your business and build pages that rank for them.

### The Keyword Sweet Spot Framework

Four attributes make a keyword worth targeting:

1. **Demand** — Are people actually searching for this? If nobody's Googling it, ranking number one has little benefit.
2. **Fit** — Where does the keyword sit in your funnel? Is it someone just learning, or someone ready to buy?
3. **Intent** — What does the searcher actually want, and can you satisfy that? If the top results are YouTube videos, you need to create a video.
4. **Keyword Difficulty** — Can you realistically rank for this given your site's current authority? A brand new website won't outrank Amazon for "running shoes," but it might rank for "best running shoes for flat feet in winter."

**Real example — PandaDoc:** The keyword "contract template" gets 300,000 searches a month. Conventional wisdom says skip it — nobody searching that is buying proposal software today. But PandaDoc consistently ranks for it. Their template directory drives hundreds of thousands of visits, with a funnel: free template download → email capture → nurture sequence → product sign-up. A top-of-funnel keyword feeding their entire pipeline.

**Real example — Counter Culture Coffee:** The keyword "pour over coffee" has 34,000 searches a month. They don't target it with a conversion-focused page. Instead, they rank with a brew guide on their blog and also rank for "pour over coffee ratio" (10,000 searches), "cold brew ratio" (6,800), and "coffee brewing methods" (5,200). All educational content. They became an authority through tutorials, building trust, and product sales followed.

A keyword without a conversion path is just wasted traffic — vanity metrics. A keyword with a conversion path could be an automated pipeline for your business.

### Keyword Research Process

**Step 1 — Keyword Ideation.** Ways to find ideas:
- Google Search Console (best source — shows exactly what people search to find your site)
- Google autocomplete suggestions
- "People also ask" boxes
- "People also search for" at the bottom of results
- Reddit — find subreddits where your customers hang out and read how they describe their problems
- Paste your sitemap into ChatGPT and ask it to identify content gaps
- Ask AI: "Give me 30 questions someone would ask before buying [product category]"
- Keyword Surfer (free Chrome extension) — shows search volume and related keywords inside Google

**Step 2 — Keyword Validation.** Take your keyword ideas and assess search volume, difficulty, business fit, and intent. AI has made this dramatically faster — what used to take days now takes minutes. You can connect an SEO data API to an AI coding tool, or use a tool like Surfer that handles validation in one screen.

**Step 3 — Keyword Clustering.** Group keywords that naturally belong together — either topically related or because Google regards them as meaning the same thing. Surfer's keyword research automates this: enter a seed topic and it maps out every cluster you should be covering.

### Keyword Prioritization: Bottom of Funnel First

Start at the bottom of the funnel and work your way upward:

**Step 1 — Money pages first.** These are pages that directly convert: product pages, service pages, pricing pages, feature pages. The keywords behind them are almost always transactional or commercial. These pages are the foundation your entire site is built on. A lot of brands go wrong by starting with high-level blog posts, writing 10 informational articles, and then wondering why nobody's buying — because they neglected their money pages.

**Step 2 — Pick one topical cluster and build it out.** Once your money pages exist, pick one cluster connected to a money page and build the full funnel for it. Example: If the money page is a coffee subscription page, build from bottom to top — the subscription page (transactional) → comparison content like "best coffee subscription box" (commercial) → educational content on brewing ratios, methods, grind size (informational). Every piece internally links to related content and to the money pages.

**Step 3 — Repeat cluster by cluster.** Finish one before starting the next. Google doesn't evaluate your page in isolation — when someone searches "how to prevent ransomware attacks," Google checks whether your site also covers "what is ransomware," "signs your network has been compromised," and "ransomware incident response checklist." If your site covers the full journey, Google trusts you more on the whole subject. This is called **topical authority** — the more comprehensively you cover a topic cluster, the easier it becomes to rank for every keyword in that cluster, including the competitive ones.

## Chapter 4: Creating Content That Ranks

Content creation happens in four phases:

1. **Research** — What's already ranking? What are competitors missing?
2. **Structure** — Build an outline that matches intent and covers the topic thoroughly.
3. **Write** — Draft content combining real expertise with optimization.
4. **Optimize** — Refine based on data, not guesswork.

### Research Phase

Before writing a single word, open the top 5 results for your target keyword and read all of them. Look for:
- What topics do they cover? (These are table stakes — you must cover them too.)
- What did they miss? (That's your opportunity.)
- What's the average length? (Your benchmark for comprehensiveness.)
- What unique angles do they take? (Where is there room to differentiate?)

### Using AI in Content Creation

AI can write really well now. The most effective approach: let AI handle structure, scaffolding, basic definitions, and sentence flow. Then add what AI can't:

- **Your experience** — what actually happened when you tried something
- **Your opinion** — what you think is overrated, underrated, or wrong
- **Original data** — your results, experiments, case studies, customer conversations
- **Expert insights** — interviews, quotes, proprietary knowledge (doesn't have to be yours — source quotes from podcasts, YouTube, books, journals)
- **Specific examples** — real businesses, real numbers, real outcomes

According to an Ahrefs study of 14 billion web pages, over 96% get zero traffic from Google. Most content fails not because it's terrible, but because it's the same as everything else. **The bar is at least five times better than what already exists for this specific search.**

Every business has unique knowledge locked in the heads of their people. If you don't have it, find expertise — interview subject matter experts, go through books and scholarly journals, go places that AI content mills can't or won't go. That will be your SEO content moat.

## Chapter 5: On-Page SEO

On-page SEO is the technical work on each individual page to help Google understand what it's about. Key elements:

**Title Tag** — Include your target keyword, keep it under 60 characters, make it compelling enough to click. Example: "Ransomware Recovery Guide for Businesses" (keyword front-loaded, tells you exactly what you're getting). For a product page, it can be more benefit-driven: "Endpoint Detection and Response Built for Every Business."

**Header Tags** — H1 is your page title (one per page, mirrors the title tag). H2s are main sections. H3s are subsections. Use keywords naturally. Google and large language models read this hierarchy to understand the structure and depth of the page.

**URL Structure** — Short, descriptive, includes the keyword. Example: `/cybersecurity-101/topic/ransomware-recovery-guide`. Clean, categorized, keyword-rich. Avoid random letters and numbers.

**Meta Description** — 150–160 characters. Not a direct ranking factor, but affects click-through rate. Write it like ad copy — action-oriented, includes the keyword, tells the searcher exactly what they'll get.

**Internal Linking** — Add contextual links inside your content to other relevant pages. This is how you connect your content clusters and how Google discovers and understands how your pages relate to each other. Don't skip this — it has one of the best effort-to-value ratios of any SEO tactic.

**Image Optimization** — Descriptive file names, alt text that describes what the image shows, compressed file sizes (WebP format recommended). Every image is an opportunity to tell Google what your page is about and to show up in image search.

## Chapter 6: Link Building

Backlinks are links from other websites to yours — votes of confidence. A link from a major industry publication carries far more weight than a link from a random web directory. Link building is the hardest part of SEO: time-consuming, often feels like cold outreach into the void, and there are no shortcuts that don't eventually get you penalized.

If you create genuinely great content with original data, unique insights, or practical tools, links come more naturally than you'd think. But here are practical tactics:

**Tactic 1 — Be the source.** Create original data, original analysis, or original resources that journalists and bloggers will cite. Counter Culture's pour-over brew guide is linked by WikiHow, Gear Patrol, Breville, and dozens of coffee blogs because it's the most thorough guide on the topic. Every business is sitting on knowledge other people want to reference — most people just never publish it.

**Tactic 2 — Journalist query platforms.** Sign up for Source of Sources (free, created by the original founder of HARO). Journalists post queries when they need expert sources. You respond with quotes and insights, and when they publish, you get a link. Takes 15 minutes a day to monitor. It's a numbers game — you might submit 100 quotes and get one link. Keep at it.

**Tactic 3 — Create linkable assets.** Free tools, calculators, templates, comprehensive guides — things that become reference material for your industry. Thanks to tools like Claude Code and Codex, it's never been easier for non-technical marketers to build real, valuable browser-based tools. A simple interactive calculator can earn links from every blog that currently references your content on that topic.

If you're just starting out, focus on creating 10–15 great pieces of content first, then start with journalist queries because it's free and takes 15 minutes a day. Links compound over time. Your job right now is to build something worth linking to.

## Chapter 7: Technical SEO

For small to medium businesses on Webflow, WordPress, Shopify, or a custom CMS, you're probably 90% of the way there already. Download **Screaming Frog SEO Spider** (free for up to 500 URLs). Point it at your site, it crawls every page, and gives you a prioritized list of issues: broken links, missing title tags, duplicate content, redirect chains, images without alt text.

If you have a physical location: claim your **Google Business Profile**, fill out every field, and get as many reviews as possible (and respond to every review). That alone can put you in the map results when people search your category near me.

The technical stuff matters, but it's not what's holding most beginners back. What's holding them back is they simply haven't published anything yet.

## Chapter 8: AI Search — The New Frontier

There's now a second search ecosystem: ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, Copilot, Claude, Grok, Google AI Mode. People are asking AI assistants for recommendations — "what's the best CRM for small businesses," "recommend a project management tool" — and these AI assistants are answering, recommending specific brands and products. If your business isn't showing up in those answers, you're invisible in an explosive new discovery channel.

AI assistants don't present information like Google. They synthesize information from across the web and generate a conversational answer. They cite sources based on authority, relevance, and how clearly your content answers the question.

**The good news:** The same things that make you rank well in Google — authoritative content, clear structure, schema markup, being cited by other sites — also help your AI visibility. Good SEO is the foundation for both.

**The challenge:** Google Search Console cannot see traffic from ChatGPT or Perplexity. If someone finds your site through an AI assistant, Search Console doesn't register the visit. What you can do is monitor whether your brand shows up when AI assistants answer questions in your space.

### How to Show Up in AI Search

1. **Create comprehensive authoritative content** with clear subheadings and direct answers to specific questions. Well-structured content with real expertise is what AI assistants cite.

2. **Build topical authority through clusters.** When your site covers a topic from every angle — the definition, the how-to, the comparison, the deep dive — AI models are more likely to recognize your brand as an authority.

3. **Earn citations from authoritative sources.** AI models weight sources that other trusted sites reference. Every backlink from a reputable site signals to AI systems that your content is worth citing. AI assistants especially favor best-of roundup articles.

4. **Check your AI visibility regularly.** AI recommendations shift dramatically over time. The brands that track it will adapt.

All of these are basically just doing good SEO. AI search optimization isn't a separate discipline — it's what happens when you do the fundamentals well.

## 30-Day Action Plan

**Week 1 — Foundation**
- Set up Google Search Console (free, essential)
- Do keyword research — find 10–15 target keywords using the Keyword Sweet Spot framework
- Analyze search intent for your top 5 keywords
- Identify your money pages — which pages on your site directly convert? Do they exist yet?

**Week 2 — Your Most Important Money Page**
- Write and publish your single most important money page (or optimize an existing one)
- Follow the content creation blueprint: research → structure → write → optimize
- Apply the on-page checklist: title, meta, headers, internal links, images
- This is the page that converts. Everything else will link back to it.

**Week 3 — Your First Funnel**
- Pick one topical cluster connected to your money page
- Publish a mid-funnel or top-of-funnel piece that supports it
- Internally link it to your money page and related pages
- Note: updating a page already ranking on page 2 or 3 is often faster than writing from scratch

**Week 4 — Promote and Monitor**
- Start responding to journalist queries on Source of Sources or Featured.com (15 min/day)
- Check your AI visibility — manually or with a tracking tool
- Review Google Search Console for early signals: impressions rising, new keywords appearing, positions trending up
- Plan your next cluster, not your next random topic

**The core message:** You probably won't see results in 30 days, but after 30 days you'll have the foundation — the right keywords, optimized content, and a system for creating more. That's further than 95% of businesses ever get.
