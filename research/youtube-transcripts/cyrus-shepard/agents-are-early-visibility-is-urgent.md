## Part 1: How AI Has Changed His Client Work

At the beginning of 2025, AI-related work was practically zero for Cyrus. By mid-2025, it's 20–30% of everything he does — specifically focused on helping clients appear in AI overviews and AI answers. His prediction: by end of 2025, it will be 70–80% of his work.

His consultancy (Zyppy) works primarily with SaaS startups and early-stage technology companies in Seattle. Five or six clients at a time.

**What he's seeing across clients right now:**

Informational content — especially blog posts that could be written by AI without any proprietary knowledge — is seeing significant click-through declines. E-commerce content and service pages are holding fine.

His test for vulnerable content: *"If you can write the article with AI without having any sort of proprietary knowledge, that's exactly the kind of content we're seeing decline."*

Content with lots of images and how-to instructions is performing better. Content with something nobody else has — proprietary data, first-hand experience, unique perspective — is doing okay.

## Part 2: AI Visibility Tracking — What's Actually Working

Cyrus runs weekly AI visibility reports for clients using Gumshoe AI (a Seattle-based tool). These tools work by asking large volumes of questions to AI search engines (Google, Perplexity, ChatGPT) pretending to be real users — "I'm a research professor, what's the best brand for X?" — and tracking how often a brand appears across those questions.

They also reverse-engineer why certain brands appear in AI answers and provide suggestions to improve content for AI overviews.

**A real result from this approach:**

For attorney clients who wanted to appear in "best attorney for X" queries, Cyrus's team moved the attorney's "about" content onto the homepage — making expertise, recognitions, and specializations clearly visible for AI to parse. The result: a clear, near-immediate improvement in Google AI overview appearances.

His honest assessment of these tools: some suggestions are very useful and actionable. Others are vague ("do better marketing everywhere") and hard to execute. But the tools are getting better and the industry is learning as it goes.

## Part 3: AI Content — The Real Guidance for Clients

Cyrus gets asked almost daily by clients: "Should we use AI for content generation?" His nuanced answer:

**Three things to consider:**

1. Google has policies against mass-produced AI content — but AI content still ranks all the time in practice.
2. Earlier in 2025, Google began instructing its quality raters (thousands of human evaluators worldwide who score web pages) to specifically look for auto-generated and AI content. Based on historical patterns, when Google starts asking quality raters to flag something, it typically gets worked into the algorithm within about a year. This means a decline in rankings for obviously AI-generated content could be coming.
3. The winning pattern isn't "use AI" or "don't use AI" — it's: **use AI to supplement content that has something AI can't replicate.**

**What AI can't replicate:**

- First-hand experience (photos of yourself at a location, opening times you verified in person)
- Proprietary data (a manufacturer's internal parts catalog, original research)
- Human interviews and original quotes
- Custom graphics and original visual assets
- Unique expertise demonstrated through specific decisions and recommendations

**The Google Helpful Content Update lesson:**

Even before AI, the HCU rewarded sites that demonstrated first-hand experience. Travel sites with original photos, personal observations, and hard-to-find details (like actual restaurant opening times) were not hit. Travel sites pumping out generic "best 10 places to visit in Orlando" articles were hit hard. That direction has continued and accelerated.

**His bottom line:** AI content is probably fine if it contains elements that AI couldn't have generated. Pure AI-generated content without a proprietary layer likely has a short shelf life.

## Part 4: The Automation Dilemma

There's a direct conflict in the current content marketing landscape:

- Social media is full of ads and posts telling marketers to automate everything with AI
- At the same time, Google is specifically devaluing the kind of content that comes from full automation

The content that's rising — getting links, getting Google's attention, earning real engagement — is the content that has a human element, is curated, and is edited. It may use AI as an assist, but it's not purely generated.

Cyrus's personal position: *"I don't want to automate everything. That's not why I got into this business. I want to create content that's awesome. I want people to link to it, to read it. I don't want it to be summarized in an AI answer. I want people to actually visit my website."*

## Part 5: Tools He Actually Uses

His core toolkit hasn't changed dramatically in the last 5–10 years:

- **Google Search Console + API** — his number one daily tool. AI has made it easier for him to connect to the API despite not being highly technical.
- **Ahrefs Content Optimizer** — a recent addition he didn't expect to love. His general complaint about content optimization tools is they make all content look the same. He thinks Ahrefs did something different here.
- **Gumshoe AI** — for weekly AI visibility tracking (discussed above)
- **A custom GPT he built in ChatGPT** — takes AI visibility suggestions from tools like Gumshoe and applies them to a piece of content to generate specific improvement recommendations. He calls it his "AI overview optimizer" and was considering making it public.
- **Gemini and ChatGPT deep research** — for research tasks, runs them in parallel

On AI agents: he uses them lightly and considers himself still in the learning phase. He's impressed with what AirOps is doing for scaling SEO tasks. For anyone interested in getting deeper into agents, he recommends following Dharmesh Shah (HubSpot founder) who has built and published thousands of agents.

## Part 6: Rapid Fire — Hot Takes

**Most overhyped AI SEO trend right now:**
Appearing as a source in AI overviews. Everyone's working hard to get cited there, but the click-through rate is low and the value is unclear. Clients who are appearing there are seeing less value than simply ranking number one used to provide. Still doing it because there's nowhere else to go — but the ROI is unproven.

**Most underrated AI use case in SEO:**
Working with embeddings and vectorization. Page embeddings are three-dimensional representations of topics — the concept behind how "man + royalty = king" and "woman + royalty = queen" relationships work in vector space. Search engines have been doing this for years. SEO tools are now making it accessible to practitioners. Cyrus sees it as a way to understand how keywords and pages relate to each other in a way that mirrors how AI search engines actually think.

**AI agents — overkill or overdue?**
Overdue. His vision: eventually AI agents will be seamless — you'll tell your phone what to do, the agents will execute, your browser will perform tasks autonomously, and you won't need special accounts or coding knowledge. It'll just be built into Google, Microsoft, and ChatGPT. Currently still rough to use, but 6–12 months away from being genuinely useful.

**One SEO task he never wants to do manually again:**
Writing content briefs. Title tag, first paragraph, second heading — tedious, time-consuming, and by the time you're done you've practically written the article. AI handles this well.

**One word for the future of SEO:**
*Confusing.* His consultancy is busier than ever — everyone needs help with visibility. But what the job looks like in two years, he genuinely doesn't know. His certainty: people will always need marketing. They'll always want to promote their brand. Marketing survives. SEO survives. The form it takes — unclear.

## Key Takeaways

- **AI work is accelerating fast** — from 0% to 20–30% of client work in months, heading toward 70–80%.
- **Informational blog content is the most vulnerable.** If AI can write it without proprietary knowledge, it's declining.
- **The winning content formula:** AI-assisted writing + human elements AI can't replicate (experience, data, interviews, original visuals).
- **AI visibility tracking tools work** — specific, targeted changes based on their recommendations produce measurable results in AI overviews.
- **Google's quality rater instructions are a leading indicator.** When they start rating for AI content detection, algorithm impact follows in ~12 months.
- **Don't automate everything.** The content earning links and rankings right now has a human, curated, edited layer — not full automation.
- **AI agents are still early** — promising but rough. Reassess in 6–12 months.
- **Core tools haven't changed much** — Google Search Console, Ahrefs, and a few targeted AI tools for specific tasks.
